On Two Entry Engines — Strongly Recommended
This is actually the architecturally correct solution to the tension we've been wrestling with. Your v2 matrix was designed for confirmed trend entry (Price > MA, All RSI > 50 — conservative, late but reliable). The v3 changes we discussed (early RSI recovery, multi-TF trough agreement, divergence detection) are inherently early detection signals. Forcing both philosophies into one scoring matrix creates the exact problem we hit — ~55 points locked behind late-firing conditions.

Here's how I'd structure it:

Engine 1: "Scout" — Fast Decision / Early Detection
Purpose: Catch recoveries before breakout confirmation
Target: Score ≥ 75 while price is still 5-15% below the breakout level
Signal philosophy: "Something is recovering — get positioned early with tight SL"
Pool A (RSI Recovery): Heavy weight (40+ pts) — Trough detection, RSI recovery momentum, multi-TF trough agreement
Pool B (Price Structure): Moderate (20-25 pts) — Only leading structure signals (higher lows forming, volume support zones), NOT Price > MA
Pool C (Confirmation): Light (10-15 pts) — Single-TF confirmation sufficient, cross-TF alignment is bonus
Bonus: RSI divergence, volume spikes at support, sector alignment
Risk model: Wider SL (3-5% below entry), smaller position size (0.5-1x standard)
Engine 2: "Sentinel" — Confirmed Trend Entry
Purpose: Enter only after trend re-establishment is verified
Target: Score ≥ 80 with multiple confirmation layers
Signal philosophy: "Trend is confirmed — deploy full capital"
Pool A (RSI Recovery): Standard weight (25-30 pts) — RSI must already be > 50
Pool B (Price Structure): Heavy (30-35 pts) — Price > 21/50 MA, higher highs/lows, structure confirmed
Pool C (Confirmation): Heavy (25-30 pts) — Cross-TF alignment required, all TFs bullish
Bonus: Strong volume trend, sector/index confirmation, multi-week consistency
Risk model: Tighter SL (1.5-2.5%), full position size (1-1.5x standard)
How They Work Together
text

Price drops → RSI troughs → Scout fires (≥75, early entry)

                        SL hit? → Exit, wait

                                ↓
                        Price breaks out → Sentinel fires (≥80)

                        Scout position → Scale up to full size
                        OR fresh Sentinel entry
Key advantage: You never have to compromise scoring weights between "catch it early" and "be sure it's real." Each engine is optimized for its own job. Implementation Suggestion
Keep them as separate scoring matrices (not conditional branches in one matrix) because:

Independent backtesting — you can validate each engine's win rate separately
Independent tuning — Scout parameters won't contaminate Sentinel and vice versa
Live flexibility — you can enable/disable either engine per stock or market regime
The classification labels (Pilot Buy / Standard / Full Entry) can remain the same, but the meaning of each label differs by engine
Bottom line: The two-engine architecture isn't just a workaround — it's the proper framework. Your Apollo analysis was essentially building the Scout engine already. The v2 matrix is your Sentinel. Now they just need to be formalized as separate, independently optimizable systems.
